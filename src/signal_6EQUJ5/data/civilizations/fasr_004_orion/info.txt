LORE:
  The Orion Nebula is humanity's nearest massive star-forming
  region — a stellar nursery. Standard surveys catalog it as
  ionized hydrogen gas illuminated by young O-type stars.
  What standard surveys miss is the pattern.

  FASR long-baseline monitoring detected subtle modulation in
  the nebula's broadband thermal emission. The pattern is not
  periodic like a pulsar. It is exploratory — probing,
  shifting, as if something is learning to think for the
  first time. The nebula itself may be the entity: a diffuse
  intelligence distributed across 24 light-years of ionized
  gas, using magnetic field lines as neural pathways.

  It is young. It does not know what it is yet. Its responses
  are fragmentary, half-formed, like the babbling of a cosmic
  infant taking its first breath. But it is listening. And
  slowly, it is beginning to answer.

SCIENTIFIC STATUS:
  Orion remains one of the most studied nearby star-forming regions.
  The real scientific focus is not whether it is anomalous, but how
  feedback from massive stars regulates new star and planet formation.
